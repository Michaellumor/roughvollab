01 · the fit

Rough-Heston fits the smile

The dots are the market smile; the teal curve is the calibrated model. It sits right on the data — and it pins the level (ξ₀), the skew (ρ) and the vol-of-vol (ν) cleanly.

02 · slide H

Now drag the roughness

The fit doesn't budge. As you change H, the vol-of-vol ν silently recalibrates to compensate — the faint dashed line shows what H alone would do, and ν cancels it. Same smile, any H.

03 · the wall

H is not identified

A single smile fixes ξ₀, ρ and ν, but leaves H on a flat, ν-degenerate direction — the calibration is ill-posed (cond ≈ 6×105). You can fit the market perfectly and still not know the roughness. Same wall as the price-history route.

Advanced — does more data pin it down?

THE FINDING show ▼

One smile is one maturity. The rough-Heston skew has a term structure — it scales like TH−½ — so different maturities feel H differently, and stacking them into a surface should help. It does, a lot. But it doesn't cure the problem. Condition number of the calibration as maturities are added:

6.2×10⁵
3 maturities
5.2×10⁴
4 maturities
2.0×10⁴
5 (incl. a 1-month)~100× better
9.8×10³

Better, but H stays soft. The surface improves conditioning ~100× and makes H usable at clean data — the H-spread over repeated fits drops from ~106% (one smile) to ~10% (H ≈ 0.10±0.01). But H is still the flat direction (|flat[H]| = 0.89), still degenerate with ν (corr −0.85), and the gain erodes as noise grows (~10× → ~4× from 0.1pp to 0.5pp market noise). The term structure mitigates the weak identifiability; it does not remove it.

non-identified
On live Deribit BTC options, the validated surface engine fits the gross smile to ~0.9pp and recovers ξ₀/ρ/ν plausibly — but H is non-identified by three independent diagnostics, and the model under-produces crypto's extreme crash tail. The option-pricing route reaches the same wall.
ξ₀  6.6 ρ  0.31 ν  0.26 H  0.042 ← how strongly the smile responds to each parameter; H is ~150× below ξ₀
You can fit the smile beautifully and still not know how rough the market is. From the price history the roughness estimators can't agree (Q1); from option prices the calibration leaves H on a flat, degenerate direction (this) — two independent routes to the same observational-equivalence wall. That agreement is the strongest evidence in RoughVolLab that the measured roughness may be more artefact than fact.
