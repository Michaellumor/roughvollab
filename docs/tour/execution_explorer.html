01 · the benchmark

Almgren–Chriss is the bar

The horizontal teal line is the Almgren–Chriss optimum — the schedule that best trades impact cost against timing risk. Anything above the line beats it; anything below is worse. The line is the bar.

02 · add vol-timing

React to the volatility

θ makes the schedule speed up when vol is high (θ > 0) or slow down (θ < 0). Slide it away from zero and the curve drops below the line — the vol-timing makes execution worse, not better.

Advanced — the edge that wasn't

THE FINDING show ▼

A vol-timing policy has one honest job here: beat Almgren–Chriss on the matched-risk frontier. It doesn't. But the story of how that was established is the point — because the very first run said the opposite.

+1.52 → fake
Phase 1's first run flashed a +1.52 “edge” at H = 0.10. Two tells exposed it: it was +2.21 at H = 0.49 — bigger when the market is smooth, so it cannot be a roughness effect — and it was ~60× the entire trading cost, impossible for a matched-risk saving. Cause: a global renormalisation that, through the ρ = −0.70 leverage, let the schedule covertly peek at future price moves. The fix — causal, per-step renormalisation — removed it.

The bar was committed before any numbers. Beat AC only if: (i) the matched-risk front sits below AC by more than the seed s.e. at several risk levels; (ii) the winning θ is clearly nonzero; (iii) the edge is sign-stable across seeds {5, 11, 23}; (iv) SANITY — it must not beat AC at H = 0.49. With look-ahead removed, the honest gap is −0.025 — per-seed [−0.034, −0.017, −0.023], about 5 s.e. worse, with no edge growing with roughness. Kill-switch fired: deep RL was not pursued.

G-X1: simulated AC = analytic AC to 0.7% roughness moves aggregate risk only 0.95× ← so any edge had to come from path-dependent vol-timing, not a different risk level — and it isn't there
Optimal execution is the exploit side of the question, and the answer matches the rest of the audit: roughness is hard to identify from price history (Q1) and from option surfaces (Q4), it isn't cheaply priceable by multilevel Monte Carlo (Q2), and here it gives no tradeable execution edge — a simple vol-aware policy cannot beat classical Almgren–Chriss, and the one time it seemed to, the discipline caught the artifact. Rough or not, you can't turn it into money at the desk.
