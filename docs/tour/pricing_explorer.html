Can you price it cheaply?

Pricing an option under rough volatility means heavy Monte Carlo. The celebrated speed-up is multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) — simulate on coarse-to-fine grids and telescope. But it only pays off if the correction between grids shrinks fast enough as you refine. Drag the roughness below and watch whether it does.

Advanced — what actually prices it cheaply

THE FINDING show ▼

If refining grids doesn't pay (β < γ), the fix isn't more levels — it's less noise per path. Conditioning on the variance path (a geometric-Asian control variate) removes a large share of the noise analytically, on a single grid — and it helps standard MC more than it helps MLMC. So the honest winner is single-grid, not multilevel. Cost to reach a fixed accuracy at H ≈ 0.1 (matched levels, seeds 11/99/1234):

the decisive, κ-invariant cost ratio cond-stdMC / cond-MLMC. It sits below 1 — so even after conditioning, going multilevel costs more than staying single-grid. Turbocharging beats telescoping.

A sharper engine helps the winner, not MLMC. Swapping the κ=0 core for exact near-cell (κ=1) integration closes the fine-path variance from 0.853 to 0.9996, making conditional single-grid MC a further ~1.3–1.5× cheaper. That sharpens the constant, not the rate — β is still ≈ 2H, so the verdict is untouched.

The honest headline: for arithmetic-Asian options under rough Bergomi, MLMC does not earn its place. The variance rate β ≈ 2H is simply too small — coarse and fine rough paths won't couple, so telescoping saves almost nothing and naive MLMC even loses to plain Monte Carlo. Cheap pricing exists — as single-grid turbocharging, not multilevel. That is the fact-checked answer to “can you price it cheaply?”
