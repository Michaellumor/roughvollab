Rough volatility · teaching module · Layer 1c — identifiability

Can you measure the
roughness back?

You can set a true roughness to build a market. But with real data, nobody hands you the answer — you have to estimate it back from what you observe. The catch: different estimators give different answers, and they disagree most exactly where markets live. Set a true H below, and watch three standard estimators try to recover it.

01 · set the truth

Start at H = 0.5

The green marker is the true roughness. At 0.5 (ordinary Brownian motion) all three estimators land near it — they agree. Roughness is identifiable here.

02 · go rough

Drag H down toward 0.1

Watch the three coloured markers spread apart and drift off the true value — two bias up, one biases down. The rougher the truth, the worse they disagree.

03 · the trap

Watch them straddle 0.5

In the rough regime the estimators can sit on both sides of Brownian — some say “rough”, some “smooth”. When they can't even agree on that, the roughness is not identifiable. That ambiguity is the finding.

Advanced — the identifiability map, and where real markets sit

THE FINDING show ▼

Sweeping the true H across its whole range and recording where the estimators agree (identifiable) versus where they straddle 0.5 (ambiguous) draws the identifiability map. The green band is where roughness can be recovered; the red band is where it can't. Then place real assets on it by their measured H. They land in the red.

Identifiability across the roughness range

This is the core result of the roughness audit: the three estimators carry different, intrinsic small-H biases — GJR and Cont–Das up, MF-DFA down — so a single Ĥ is a trap. The honest read is the spread. And real assets (BTC, ETH, S&P 500) all sit deep in the rough, non-identifiable band — precisely where the estimators can't agree whether the series is even rough or smooth. So the “markets are rough (H≈0.1)” claim rests on exactly the regime where roughness cannot be reliably identified. That is the fact-or-artefact question RoughVolLab exists to referee.
